# Project Management Is About Managing Contradictions

The title of this book is "Delivering the Impossible." It's impossible to deliver the impossible. But it somehow seems more exciting, more of a challenge. "Delivering the Possible" doesn't sound like such a great title. Project management is riddled with contradictions. This is because project management is about managing the conflict between what we want and what we get can get. 

The main contradiction that I talk about in this book is the contradiction between the method of getting money, support, value and approval for a project and the method of implementing that project so that it can generate money, value and approval. One thing I find about these contradictions is that they very elusive, so I'm going to have another go at stating this one. Maybe by telling a story. 

Let's meet Geoff. Geoff has an idea for a project. Geoff puts some ideas into a slide presentation a delivers his presentation to the board. To Geoff's surprise the board give Geoff £3M pounds and a year to turn his idea into a working project. 

What Geoff doesn't know, is that the board have seen analysis that their core business isn't getting return on investment that it was. Geoff's idea is in a field that is moving fast and looks like it might be a winner. Is £3M pounds a lot of money? It is to some people, but in terms of the overall accounts of this particular business, it's not a lot of money. They can afford to lose it (although of course, they'd rather not).

But now, Geoff *really* has a problem. In a year's time he needs the project to be online and working - and at least *look like* it can pay the board's three million pounds back. So he starts to spend the money, gets himself a nice big office. That takes longer than he thought, several months. This also takes longer than he thought. And he really has no idea what the right kind of people are to hire to build his idea. Already, he's three months into project, he's spent nearly a third of the money! And he's got precisely nothing.

Then the questions come. Which technology should we be using for the font end? Who are we going to work with on the back end? In the market there are people offering what we're offering already. How should we compete? Some of these are technical questions that he can hand off to the new technical people that he's hired. I mean what the hell is a front end? He didn't hire the most expensive people he could find, he hired people who were slightly cheaper, simply because he couldn't *believe* how much the most expensive people cost. Some of these questions, he knows are for him, and he also knows that he can't provide answers. He realises something that he's only vaguely had to deal with before he got this "big break." He's a *salesman*. Putting together business models, hiring technical people, those aren't his skillsets. 

Somebody had told him that the way to do a software development project is using Agile, and if you're going to do Agile you need to have someone called a "Product Owner." So he hired one of those. He literally had no idea what the things were on the guy's CV, so he picked the guy he thought would be toughest with the team. Unfortunately this guy is being pretty tough now with *him*.
