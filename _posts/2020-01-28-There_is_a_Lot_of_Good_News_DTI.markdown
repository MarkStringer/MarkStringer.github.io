# There is a *Lot* of Good News

What if you did pick up this book thinking that it *would* do what it exaplained on the cover and explain how to deliver the impossible? Or what if you thought that it might be like many other books on project management, explain some kind of rigid (yet, also, somehow vague) process and claim that if you follow that process closely, you are guranteed to succeed. If you expected either (or both!) of those things from this book, you might be feeling let down at this point. 

But don't be. There's a *lot* of good news. 

## You're better at a lot of this than I am

One of the uncomfortable things that I've come to see as a result of seeing what project management is *really* about and what it is that I'm *really* doing, is that a lot of the skills that are needed for good project management aren't things that I'm particularly good at.

### Talking to C-Level people
I know you're better at this than I am. How do I know? Because I'm terrible at it. I wish I were good at it but it just isn't my thing.

### Identifying Issues that are Blocking the Team and Getting them Fixed
I'm *OK* at this - I'd give myself a 6 or 7/10. But there are a lot of people who are a *lot* better at this. I know *lots* of better at this than me. 

### Talking to Users and Customers
At least with this one I can claim that this isn't my skill set. But what I've come to realise is that without ongoing, if not constant contact with customers and users throughout the development of the software, the product value stream doesn't get built. Also, the subtleties of the funding value stream and how transfer to the product value stream can be managed don't show themselves.

### All the Technical Stuff
If you've got any technical facility, you've probably got more than me.

### So what *ARE* you good at?
> I don't solve people's problems, I just televise them - ***Jerry Springer (In Jerry Springer the Opera)***

I'm not special, there are a lot of Scrum Masters, Delivery Managers, Agile Project Managers who can do exactly what I do. How do I know? Because when I talk to them about the projects they're working on, or they talk to me about the projects that they're working on, we talk about the same things: "Ambitious timescales", "Lack of access to customers", "Lack of access to subject matter experts", "Lack of access to environments." Long sign-off or approval processes.

None of us are special (OK, some of us are *really* special, but for very different reasons). But what we are doing is using mostly Scrum and mostly Extreme Programming methods, to get us to prioritise what needs to be delivered, throw up the problems that might stop those things from being delivered, and then having solved those *deliver* software that can actually deliver value.

Oscar Wilde really did say "We are all in the gutter, but some of us a gazing at the stars." What's my superpower? ***Looking in the gutter!*** Because I know that the only way to actually survive is to clean any blockages out of the gutter an make sure everything is flowing smoothly.
