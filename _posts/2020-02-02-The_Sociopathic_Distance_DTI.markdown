# The Sociopathic Distance

When this piece is in a final publishable form, that probably won't be the title. Because this is a particularly prickly notion. 

I'm just going to come out and say it. Projects go well, projects go badly. A true sociopath will make sure that they are closely associated with any project that goes well, they will also make sure that they are nowhere to be found when a project goes badly. We all know a person like this. We all know a project that has had torrid troubled times and then, when it turns around, suddenly people start appearing who claim to have been involved in the project all along. 

Similarly, we all know of projects where, as it becomes abundantly obvious that the project is going to be a failure, there are all of a sudden very few people who can remember that they were ever involved in it. 

And there's a general view that this is a bad way of behaving (for a long, long, time, that was my view). But is it?

Moving towards praise and avoiding blame, that's a sensible thing to do, isn't it?

What's the alternative? Avoiding praise? Seeking out blame? Taking only the praise and blame that's due to you?

OK - let me tell you a story. I worked on a project that was successful, delivered on time, delivered on budget and well-received by its users. Your estimation may vary as to how unusual that is. Let me tell you this extra detail. It was a government project. Yes, it's unusual. It happens. But not very often.

A really important part of why the project was successful was the product owner. She had been a clerk of the court (they were the people who would be the users). The official line, conventional, Agile thinking is that the product owner should be close to the team. But this product owner didn't do that. In the early days of the project, when the project was wrestling with the kind of "blockers" that most government projects were dealing with in the mid-teens of the twentieth century - lack of access to environments, insistence that projects could be done for nearly free with open source off-the-shelf software - the product owner got herself an early demonstrator of the software on her laptop and took it on the road to show to her former colleagues. For a while she turned up for the key meetings (planning, show and tell) but then after a few, very awkward early meetings where the team wasn't showing much progress, she started to send a deputy.

But this was a well-run Agile project, so the issues that the team needed to tackle came to light early in the process when there was enough time and resources to address them. The effort required to customise the open source software was sized and extra resources agreed. The product owner started turning up to show and tells, planning, and even sometimes stand-ups.

As the project started to show itself to be a success, the product owner started to associate herself more closely with it. 

It might be tempting to disapprove of such behaviour, but there are several reasons that this kind of behaviour might be worthy of approval.

The first is that when she was away from the team this particular product owner was doing exactly the right thing in terms of manipulation of value streams. She was spending as much time as she could with the people who would actually use the system that was getting developed and she was showing them prototypes of the software. So, while the product owner was away from the team she was doing exactly the kind of "value stream irrigation" that is necessary to make a smooth transition from funding value stream, to product value stream. 

The second is that, if the project had been a disaster, in terms of her career she'd done the right thing. She'd kept on good terms with her constituency, and if things had gone wrong, she would have been able to shrug her shoulders, and be disappointed but nobody would have been tempted to put the blame on her.

In his book "The Innovation Algorithm", Genrich Altshuller identifies an essential inventive step as being the removal of a contradiction. He also admits that many successful inventions are less transformational, but still valuable because they manage a contradiction. 

We've already discussed one contradiction, which is central to this book: the process that got the money to start a project isn't the process that will get project done so that it can pay back that money.

This is contradiction is certainly related, but managing it is different. Put bluntly, just to get it out on the page, it's like this.

***Someone who instigates projects who has no reponsbility for failure and take unwarranted responsibility for success will be an utter disaster.***

But 

***If all captains go down with their ships, either captains will be very, very, careful, or there won't be many captains***

Projects are a risky business.
