# Why are you doing this?

Why would I write a book? Well, for some reason, I feel embarrassed to admit it, but I think it might be quite nice to have *written* a book. That's one reason. And that is very much a "switch all the way over to done" value. 

Why else might you want to write a book. Since he idea first came into my mind, one of the reasons for wanting to write a book is that my experience of project management is that it is a messy, emotional and *unreasonable* business. This is in sharp contrast to most of the descriptions of project management methods and cetainly all of the training courses.

So, this is a value for me, but it still seems like a less vain and self-centred value. I wanted to write a book about project management that in some way acknowledged the experiences that I'd had managing actual projects.

Lastly, but most importantly, because this is where this book might, will be valuable to other people, I wanted to write a book that talked about project managment in a way that made it easy for smart people to do smart things rather than dumb things when they are delivering projects, or asking other people to deliver projects. 

Because most of the skills that I talk about in this book, "emotional management", "mustering resources", "talking to C-level people", "talking to users", "product management" not to mention "software development" and "dev ops" are skills that I either don't have, or don't have in ambundance. But what I *do* have is the ability to see beyond the dream which has attracted the funding for a project to the reality of what needs to be done to delivery and see the gap between those two things. My thinking is that if I can explain to talented people the nature of that gap, why it's there and how to go about bridging it, then cleverer people than me will do far better than I can at move projects from the dream state to the real state where they are either delivering value for their organisations or are being swiftly forgotten.
