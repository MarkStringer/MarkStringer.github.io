# The Air Steward and The Debt Collector

> "If you can keep you head when all about you are losing theirs and blaming it on you." - Rudyard Kipling.

Spending money on software development is an expensive business. And because it's expensive it can be risky for the bottom line of a company, for the career and reputation of the people who have championed the project. At certain points in a project the news can be bad, and people can react to that new emotionally.  

Flying isn't really a risky business. There's a risk involved but, statistically, it's very small. Still people do get very upset about flying. And that upset can show itself in many ways. The job of the air steward is to manage the emotions of their passengers. When their passengers get upset to soothe them, and also, above all, to maintain an appearance of calm themselves.

Being an air steward is, obviously, a job. And the writer Arlie Russell Hochschild has made a very good case for a substantial part of that job being the work of managing emotions - "emotional labour" as she calls it. Emotional labour is about helping others to maintain - or regain - a positive emotional state, whilst at the same time, at least outwardly, maintaining a positive emotional state yourself. 

Once you're familiar with the idea of emotional labour, you start to see it everywhere. You see it in other types of work and professions, doctors, lawyers, waiters, shop assistants, hair dressers. But you also see it in your own life, both in your personal life when pretending to enjoy yourself at some awfully boring social occasion, or maintaining a cheery demeanour while visiting the sick. But also, of course, you see it in your work life. 

"Keeping your head while others are losing theirs" is definitely part of being a project manager. Hochschild's book also deals with the careers and the occasions when certain professions have to project a more negative emotion. Debt collectors can't be swayed by sob stories. Doctors, lawyers and accountants have to give their clients bad news.

This is definitely a part of being a project manager. And in books, presentations, training courses and discussions of the various Agile methodologies this is rarely, if at all, discussed.

And I'll admit that at least some of the time, I'm not good at it. I was kicked off of a government project after two weeks, I was subcontracting to another company, and it nearly lost me my job with them (I'd only been working with them for the same length of time). For a long time after this very negative experience, I would explain that experience as being the result of pointing out that the project couldn't possibly be delivered by the deadline. That was certainly part of it. But the other part was that the naive, and scared product owner, who was starting to realise that the project couldn't be delivered by the deadline, said something which was to my mind idiotic and infuriating in the stand-up and rather than keeping my head, I got annoyed in response. I was out the next day. I've never admitted that before.

It took six months to kick me off another project after I had a very similar conversation with a naive, inexperienced (are you starting to see a pattern?) project director who wanted me to promise that a project would be released by a specific date. 

Only now, as I write this, am I starting to see that the governing factor in whether these "crunch" meetings go well or not might well be the level of experience of the people who are being told the bad news.

In the successful project that I worked on recently, there was a similar "crunch" meeting. Quietly, over a period of several weeks, I'd put together a burn-up chart showing how much work there was and how much work we had done. For those of you who know about Agile, the way that I did this was a bit unconventional. We didn't really have a backlog of all the stories that needed doing - we were pretty creating the stories for a sprint at the beginning (in the middle) of a sprint. And we weren't estimating anything. So what I did was have a meeting with the team where we made a list of all the epics that needed to have been done by the time that we went live. I think that might have been one meeting. 

Then in another meeting I got the team to estimate the complexity of these stories using fibonacci comparison. That gave me a total for the backlog. Then, once a fortnight I had a "mid sprint check" where I asked the team to tell me roughly what percentage "Done" each of the stories was. 

This project had a hard deadline. Pretty much on one day, the old system was going to be turned off and our new one was going to be turned on (I know, I know this is crazy, in the end, we did mitigate this with a phased delivery).
